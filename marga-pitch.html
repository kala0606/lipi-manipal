Proposal 02 · Manipal library

MARGA

Every sign is grown from its own words.

A library’s signage is usually the last thing designed and the first thing ignored. MARGA makes it the artwork. One generative engine produces every sign the building needs — from the entrance monolith to the smallest bay label — and each sign is generated from the words it carries. 500–599 doesn’t just point to science; it draws itself from those digits. The classification system becomes the visual grammar — the catalogue, read differently.

Etched into wood and glass, the work is distributed through the whole building. Nobody sees all of it at once: you find it the way you find books, by moving through the stacks. Reading extends beyond the page.

One system · three movements

Every sign does one of three jobs

It moves you, it marks a place, or it asks you to pause. Each job has its own engine. All three share one discipline — a single etched line — so the whole building reads as one hand.

marga · move

Directional signs — directories, blades, range signs, bay labels — carry one unbroken line walked through a seeded labyrinth: a small map of what a library is. Stacks, aisles, one path through.

sthana · dwell

Place signs — the entrance, rooms, desks — are a single closed line grown by slow accumulation until it fills its sign. Hierarchy is time: the entrance is grown for hours, a desk sign in minutes.

virama · pause

Notices — quiet zones, hours, house rules — settle. Sediment lines of an imagined terrain lie behind the words: strata, like knowledge, laid down slowly.

Deterministic · reproducible

Same words, same sign, forever

Every sign is seeded by the text it carries. There is no artwork file to archive and no artist to call back: any sign, decades from now, can be re-etched exactly from its own words. Rename the room and the sign becomes a new one — the system produces work for as long as the library changes.

Exhaustive

The catalogue — thirteen collaterals, one hand

Every format the building needs, from monument to label, generated by the same engine at the appropriate scale. Adding a fourteenth is a template, not a redesign.

In situ

In the building

The system in place — birch in the stacks, glass at the thresholds. Every sign below is the engine’s own output, mocked into the architecture.

Material · method

Etched, not printed

Signage has to outlive paper. The linework is vector-etched and the lettering engraved into birch or walnut ply, or frost-etched into glass — materials that age with a building instead of fading on it. This page previews both: light is wood, dark is glass — switch the theme to walk between them.

  • lineworksingle-width vector etch, 0.35 mm tool path — the same polylines the engine draws
  • letteringraster-engraved. Any typeface can be etched or cut; sign lettering here is a plain formal sans with serif numerals, as a placeholder — the faces are the committee’s choice
  • hierarchyetch time. A bay label is minutes of machine time; the entrance monolith is hours. Importance is made of time, not size alone
  • longevityno inks, no prints, no fading. A damaged sign is re-etched from its words — identical, because the words are the file
Execution

From one floor to the whole building

  • pilotone floor: a directory, six range signs, two room plaques, one quiet plate — enough to live with the system before committing
  • buildingthe full catalogue across all floors, materials mixed deliberately — wood in the stacks, glass at thresholds
  • afterwardsthe engine stays with the library. New sections, renamed rooms, future wings: every future sign reissued from the same grammar, by anyone
  • open choicesthe typeface, the material palette, and the words themselves — offered to the committee, as they should be
